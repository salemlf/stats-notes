We can use the bivariate method of transformation by rewriting $U_1 = Y_1 / (Y_1 + Y_2)$ and letting $U_2 = Y_1 + Y_2$, then finding $f_{U_1, U_2}(u_1, u_2)$, then integrating it with respect to $u_2$ to find the marginal density of $U_1$.
Let $h: \mathbb{R}_{> 0}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ be defined by

$$
\begin{align*}
    (u_1, u_2) & = h(y_1, y_2) = (h_1(y_1, y_2), h_2(y_1, y_2)) = \left(\frac{y_1}{y_1 + y_2}, y_1 + y_2 \right)\\
    \implies (y_1, y_2) & = h^{-1}(u_1, u_2) = (h_1^{-1}(u_1, u_2), h_2^{-1}(u_1, u_2)) = (u_1 u_2, (1 - u_1)u_2).
\end{align*}
$$

Assuming $(v_1, v_2), (w_1, w_2) \in \mathbb{R}_{> 0}^2$,

$$
\begin{align*}
    & h(v_1, v_2) = h(w_1, w_2)\\
    \Longleftrightarrow & \left(\frac{v_1}{v_1 + v_2}, v_1 + v_2 \right) = \left(\frac{w_1}{w_1 + w_2}, w_1 + w_2 \right)\\
    \Longleftrightarrow & \left(\frac{v_1}{v_1 + v_2} \cdot (v_1 + v_2), v_1 + v_2 \right) = \left(\frac{w_1}{w_1 + w_2} \cdot (v_1 + v_2), w_1 + w_2 \right)\\
    \Longleftrightarrow & \left(v_1, v_1 + v_2 \right) = \left(\frac{w_1}{w_1 + w_2} \cdot (w_1 + w_2), w_1 + w_2 \right)\\
    \Longleftrightarrow & \left(v_1, v_1 + v_2 - v_1 \right) = \left(w_1, w_1 + w_2 - v_1 \right)\\
    \Longleftrightarrow & \left(v_1, v_2 \right) = \left(w_1, w_1 + w_2 - w_1 \right)\\
    \Longleftrightarrow & \left(v_1, v_2 \right) = \left(w_1, w_2 \right),\\
\end{align*}
$$

thus, $h$ is one-to-one by definition.
Then the Jacobian of $h$ is

$$
J = \det
\begin{bmatrix}
    \frac{\partial h_1^{-1}}{\partial u_1} & \frac{\partial h_1^{-1}}{\partial u_2}\\
    \frac{\partial h_2^{-1}}{\partial u_1} & \frac{\partial h_2^{-1}}{\partial u_2}
\end{bmatrix} = \det
\begin{bmatrix}
    u_2 & u_1\\
    -u_2 & 1 - u_1
\end{bmatrix} = u_2 (1 - u_1) - u_1 (-u_2) = u_2.
$$

The last part we need is $f_{Y_1, Y_2}$, which will be the following by the independence of $Y_1$ and $Y_2$:

$$
f_{Y_1, Y_2}(y_1, y_2) =
\begin{cases}
    e^{-(y_1 + y_2)}, & y_1 > 0, y_2 > 0,\\
    0, & \text{elsewhere.}
\end{cases}
$$

Thus,

$$
\begin{align*}
    f_{U_1, U_2}(u_1, u_2) & = f_{Y_1, Y_2}(h_1^{-1}(u_1, u_2), h_2^{-1}(u_1, u_2)) |J|\\
    & = |u_2| \cdot
    \begin{cases}
        e^{-(u_1 u_2 + (1 - u_1)u_2)}, & u_1 u_2 > 0, (1 - u_1)u_2 > 0,\\
        0, & \text{elsewhere}
    \end{cases}\\
    & =
    \begin{cases}
        u_2 e^{-u_2}, & 0 < u_1 < 1, 0 < u_2\\
        0, & \text{elsewhere.}
    \end{cases}
\end{align*}
$$

The support shown above can be found via the following sequence of steps:

$$
\begin{align*}
    0 < u_1 u_2 & \\
    \text{and } 0 < (1 - u_1)u_2 & \Longleftrightarrow 0 < u_2 - u_1 u_2\\
    & \Longleftrightarrow u_1 u_2 < u_2\\
    & \Longleftrightarrow 0 < u_1 u_2 < u_2\\
    & \Longleftrightarrow 0 < u_1 < 1 \text{ and } 0 < u_2.
\end{align*}
$$

Thus,

$$
f_{U_1}(u_1) =
\begin{cases}
    \int_{u_2 = 0}^{u_2 = \infty} u_2 e^{-u_2} du_2\\
    = -u_2 e^{-u_2} \biggr|_{u_2 = 0}^{u_2 = \infty} - \int_{u_2 = 0}^{u_2 = \infty} -e^{-u_2} du_2\\
    = 0 + (0)e^{-(0)} - \left[e^{-u_2} \right]_{u_2 = 0}^{u_2 = \infty}\\
    = -\left[0 - e^{-(0)} \right] = -(-1) = 1, & 0 < u_1 < 1,\\
    0, & \text{elsewhere.}
\end{cases}
$$
